What is the Old World?

Although this sounds somewhat paradoxical, the discovery of the New World marked the emergence of the Old. Five centuries have passed since then, but the Old World is a concept that is still used today. What value did you put in it before? What does it mean today?

Definition of the term

The Old World is that part of the land that Europeans knew in the Middle Ages before the discovery of the American continent. The division was conditional and was based on the position of the land relative to the sea. Merchants and travelers believed that there are three parts of the world: Europe, Asia, Africa. Europe lies in the north, Africa in the south, and Asia in the east. Subsequently, when the data on the geographical division of the continents became more accurate and complete, it was found that only Africa was a separate continent. However, the ingrained views turned out to be not so easy to defeat, and all 3 parts of the world continued to be traditionally mentioned separately.



Sometimes the name Afro-Eurasia is used to determine the territorial mass of the Old World. In fact, this is the largest continental massif - the supercontinent. It is home to approximately 85 percent of the world's population.

A period of time

Speaking of the Old World, they often mean not just a specific geographical location. These words carry information about a specific historical period, culture and then made discoveries. We are talking about the Renaissance, when medieval asceticism and theocentrism are replaced by ideas of natural philosophy and experimental science.

The attitude of a person to the world is changing. Gradually, from the toy of a whole host of gods, who have the power to control human life according to their whims and whims, a person begins to feel like the master of his earthly home. He strives for new knowledge, which leads to a number of discoveries. Attempts are being made to explain the structure of the surrounding world using mechanics. Improving measuring instruments, including navigation. One can already trace the origin of such natural sciences as physics, chemistry, biology and astronomy, which are replacing alchemy and astrology.



The changes that took place then gradually paved the way for expanding the borders of the known world. They served as a prerequisite for the discovery of new lands. Brave travelers traveled to uncharted lands, and their stories inspired even more daring and risky ventures.

Historical journey of Christopher Columbus

In August 1492, three well-equipped ships under the command of Christopher Columbus sailed from Palos harbor to India. This was the year of the discovery of America, but the famous discoverer himself never found out that he had discovered a continent unknown to Europeans before. He was sincerely sure that he had made all four of his expeditions to India.



The journey from the Old World to new lands took three months. Unfortunately, it was neither cloudless, nor romantic, nor disinterested. The admiral hardly restrained the subordinate sailors from the riot in the first voyage, and the main driving force for opening new territories was greed, thirst for power and vanity. These ancient vices brought from the Old World subsequently brought much suffering and grief to the inhabitants of the American continent and nearby islands.

Christopher Columbus himself did not get what he wanted. Going on the first voyage, he prudently tried to protect himself and secure his future. He insisted on concluding a formal agreement, according to which he received the noble title, the rank of admiral and vice-king of the newly discovered lands, as well as the percentage of income received from the above lands. And although the year of the discovery of America was supposed to be a ticket to a secured future for a discoverer, after some time Columbus fell into disgrace and died in poverty, not receiving what was promised.

Wine cards replace geographic

If in the field of geography, terminology, including the division of continents into the New and Old Worlds, is already a relatively rare occurrence, then among winemakers such definitions are still held in high esteem. There are stable expressions: "wine of the Old World" and "wine of the New World." The difference between these drinks is not only in the place where the grapes grow and the location of the winery. They are rooted in the same differences that are characteristic of continents.

So, wines of the Old World, mostly produced in France, Italy, Spain, Germany and Austria, are distinguished by their traditional taste and delicate elegant bouquet. And the wines of the New World, which are famous for Chile, Argentina, Australia and New Zealand, are brighter, with obvious fruity notes, but somewhat losing in refinement.



Old World in the modern sense

Today, the term "Old World" is mainly applied to states located in Europe. In the vast majority of cases, neither Asia nor Africa is taken into account. So, depending on the context, the expression "Old World" can include either as many as three parts of the world, or only European states.
