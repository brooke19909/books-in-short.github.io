What is Red Pine: location and history

The article will discuss what Red Pine is. All questions regarding this place will be sorted out in detail, and its location and origin itself will be painted. That is why those interested are simply obligated to read this article to the end.

Definition





Red pine is a street in modern Moscow. However, this was not always the case. Before that, it was just an extensive summer cottage village, where small houses with the same small plots of land were located. Some 50 years ago, Red Pine was one of the most favorite places of Muscovites, where you could grow fresh vegetables to the table, plant fruit trees and flowers. In addition, in Red Pine it was possible to spend the weekend, while in the fresh air, surrounded by stunningly beautiful nature, to relax from the hustle and bustle of the never-sleeping capital. Now, Moscow simply swallowed up the former dacha village of Red Pine, and at the moment it has turned into one of the most developing and developing areas within the city.

Location





And it is located beyond the Severyaninsky overpass and stretches from the north-east to the south-west for almost 2 km on both sides of the busy Yaroslavl highway on the north-eastern outskirts of Moscow.

Today, the former holiday village occupies a significant part of the Yaroslavl district of the capital and is actively developing, expanding its borders through numerous new buildings. The western side of the street rests on the 38th Babushkinsky microdistrict, where it passes along the railway line in the direction of Yaroslavsky Station-Pushkino.

What is this street?

Red pine, not without reason, can be called a place of striking contrasts. Here, bright almost virgin nature is adjacent to huge industrial enterprises and modern buildings, which do not spoil this unusual color.

The southern outskirts of the street is literally immersed in the emerald greenery of the pine forest of Yauzsky Forest Park, which is a great place for relaxation and walking. The territory of Elk Island, a forest tract, which is the nature conservation zone of the capital and one of its natural treasures, is even more attractive for this. This is simply an indispensable place where you can have a great time with your whole family or, conversely, wander around the well-groomed forest paths, enjoy plenty of tranquility and put your thoughts in order.





On the other hand, Red Pine in Moscow is now considered the most rapidly developing region. At the moment, it houses 74 major trading, financial, construction and other companies. Among them, one can also single out architectural workshops, the Geodetic Agency, the Independent Evaluation Bureau, and many others dealing with a wide variety of service delivery areas.

Infrastructure and Maintenance

The main transport artery of Red Pine, providing communication with other areas of Moscow, uninterrupted delivery of goods, filling outlets with necessary goods, of course, is the Yaroslavl highway. But due to the large-scale development of the street every year it becomes more and more busy. Of course, there are still no such problems as in the central regions of the capital, but they periodically arise. However, there are alternatives to the Yaroslavl highway in the form of the Severyaninskaya and Losinoostrovskaya railway stations, in addition, more recently, 2 metro lines VDNKh and Sviblovo were brought to the street, so there are simply no problems with transport and service.

History of Red Pine





Now it‚Äôs clear what the Red Pine is, its history, though not rich in events, is more than a hundred years old. The appearance of its current street is due to the growth of the then imperial capital. The first buildings here arose in 1911. These were country houses of wealthy Muscovite merchants, industrialists, and representatives of the nobility.

But after the October Revolution, land for summer cottages began to be allocated to cultural workers, medical workers and ordinary workers. In just a few years, by 1920, the cottage settlement had grown so much that it could be considered a separate settlement, although in administrative terms it was listed as Losinoostrovsky. Initially, the village was called Rostokino, in 1939 it was renamed Babushkin, and already under the name Red Pine in 1960, it became an integral part of the Russian capital.
