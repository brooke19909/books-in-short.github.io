Crochet Safety: Maybe You Didn't Know This

It is not known whether statistical calculations of astringent people all over the world were ever conducted, but it is obvious that the figure would be impressive! Of course, most girls in one way or another encountered crochet and knitting in labor lessons in schools. But to truly appreciate the merits of the most popular hobby is possible only in adulthood, when experience appears and the concept of style develops.



Interestingly, the multimillion-dollar audience wielding sharp knitting needles, hooks, needles and other sharp-cutting objects rarely thinks that there are safety rules for crocheting.

Rules? What for?

The main reason for developing safe knitting techniques was just the above-mentioned school lessons. Especially for the training program, a list of what students should and should not do with the equipment issued to them was compiled.



Basically, safety when crocheting and knitting is reduced to the fact that all the tools should be used for their intended purpose, do not poke anyone in the eye and do not test your and other people's skin for durability. All rules correspond to elementary common sense.

Crochet & Knitting Safety

In more detail about the content of safe knitting rules:

  1. Knitting needles, hooks and needles should not be brought to the face.
  2. These items should be stored in appropriate pencil cases, cases, boxes and drawers.
  3. If during the knitting process it will be necessary to work with a large number of needles, pins and knitting needles, they must be counted before and after knitting.
  4. Tools should not be sharpened too much, this may result in personal injury.
  5. Do not use objects that have rust. In addition to health hazards (in the case of scratches), they can also ruin yarn or fabric.
  6. Safety precautions when working with a crochet hook include a clause on the disposal of broken needles and pins. They need to be folded into a special container, packed in thick paper and taken out to a garbage container.
  7. If you want to chop the knitted fabric with a pin, it is pierced in three punctures. Thus, the sharp edge will always be on the visible surface. However, if they plan to try on a cleaved product, it is better to make sure that the tip of the pin is between two layers of the fabric.
  8. When working with scissors, they are always put in rings to themselves, and the blades are left closed. If necessary, the scissors should be turned in rings to the receiver.
