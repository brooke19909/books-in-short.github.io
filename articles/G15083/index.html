History

On the territory of Primorsk, archaeologists discovered the remains of the sites of ancient people of the Middle Paleolithic period. In 1800, on the banks of the Obitnaya river, serfs founded the village of the same name. In 1817, by order of the Duke of Richelieu, who at that time was the governor of the Novorossiysk Territory, the construction of the Azov port began. Initially, its construction was planned in the valley of the Obitonnaya River, but later it turned out that this place was not suitable for the construction of such a structure. The port was built on the east coast of the Berdyansk Bay (the modern territory of Berdyansk).

In 1821, the village of Obitochnoe, now Primorsk (Zaporizhzhya region), with the surrounding environs was renamed Nogaysk. In 1813 - 1814 the church of the Holy Trinity was built in connection with the victory in the war of 1812. In 1836 the church was made stone. In 1936, it was partially destroyed (the bell tower was lost). Since 1991, the church is recognized as an architectural monument. The inhabitants preserved the icon of the Kazan Mother of God, which is now in the cathedral.

In 1964, Nogaysk was renamed Primorsky, and in 1967 the town was given the status of a city. Soon the name of the city was slightly corrected in Primorsk.

sights

There are not many attractions in the city, but the buildings of the 19th century are preserved - the structure of the Ukrainian-Bulgarian Lyceum, the Museum of Local Lore and the Holy Trinity Church, which is now working and being restored.

Primorsk (Zaporizhzhya region) is famous for its underground routes. Under the city is a dense network of corridors and labyrinths. Most of the dungeon moves lead to the sea, and their entrances were in many old houses, but over time they were walled up. During World War II, residents and partisans took refuge in these crossings. It is not known where these catacombs came from. However, there are legends that even during the Turkish invasions, local residents hid in them, and thereby escaped from slavery.
