GLONASS

If a lot of things is known about the GPS system, it is well-known to everyone, then they say less about its double - GLONASS. It should be noted that it was developed back in the USSR. It uses two types of navigation signals: FDMA and CDMA.

The basis of the system are satellites in Earth orbit, which move in different orbital planes. The system is largely similar to GPS, the only difference is that the satellites do not resonate with the rotation of the planet. Because of this, the signal is more stable. But the life of the satellites is much shorter than that of GPS.

In total, 25 satellite complexes of the GLONASS-M type and two GLONASS-K are involved in orbit. In 2018, it is planned to launch the upgraded GLONASS-K2 satellite system.

Conclusion

It should be noted that in modern society, the presence of a DVR in a car is something like a rule of good taste. It is hard to imagine a motorist without this gadget.

Situations on the roads are quite delicate. And in the event of a traffic accident, the driver is simply vital to secure conclusive evidence of his innocence. Video recording is just that. Therefore, it must be of high quality. Because of this, the choice falls on the iBOX Combo F1 plus. Owner reviews are proof of this.

Taxi drivers use this device especially often - people for whom their own car is not only a means of transportation, but also a source of income.

On May 7, 2016, a legislative amendment entered into legal force, according to which, courts accept DVR materials as evidence. Earlier this was done only at the discretion of the judge.

In view of which, it is recommended that drivers in case of an accident in the presence of witnesses report that a video recorder is installed in the car. Next, remove the USB flash drive from the gadget and seal it in an envelope for all, so that there is no doubt about the authenticity of the video.





A legislative amendment applies not only to drivers in fact. For example, upon receipt of complaints by the police about hooligan actions of specific individuals in the courtyard (for example, a fight or shooting from a traumatic pistol), the materials of the video recorders of nearby cars can be used to collect materials and further initiate criminal proceedings.

Complicated cases in the analysis of insurance cases can also lead to the use of video materials not only from DVRs, but also from ordinary outdoor surveillance cameras and even cell phones.

It should also be mentioned separately that the court still has the right not to accept video surveillance materials as evidence if there are doubts about the authenticity of such materials or if there are clear signs of video editing.

The installation even provides for criminal prosecution as for the obvious falsification of evidence. This applies not only to video materials, but also to photos. Simply, if previously the materials of the video recordings could be considered by the court as information for the formation of the evidence base solely by decision of the judge, then now they are in any case accepted. But judges have the right not to consider them if the authenticity of those is suspicious.
