Briefly about devices

The name of the cameras comes from the phrase ‚Äúfull frame‚Äù. The full frame is the size of the photosensitive matrix, which is responsible for the quality of the image. The larger the matrix, the better the picture quality will be, the less noise will be with a lack of light. In cameras, the half-format size, that is, the APS-C matrix 23x15 mm, is most often used. APS-C is the generally accepted designation of matrices with crop factor (truncated size). In full-frame cameras, the dimensions of the matrix coincide with the dimensions of a 35 mm film camera (35x24 mm). Pictures taken with a full-frame camera are 1.5 times larger than with a half-format camera.

What is the popularity?

Film cameras appeared in the 19th century, but why are full-frame devices gaining popularity just now? The fact is that when the active production of digital cameras began, they most often used smaller matrices due to the too high cost of full-frame sensors. Now such matrices have become more affordable, so the demand for them is growing.

Pros and cons of full-frame cameras

As mentioned in the previous paragraph, modern half-format cameras can well compete with full-frame in picture quality, size and price. What are the benefits of full-frame photography?

  • The size and photosensitivity of the matrix help to create very high quality images with good detail.
  • Work with low noise, which is good for photographers who, for example, conduct photo hunting on rare animals.
  • The presence of serial shooting, this allows you to catch the natural movement.
  • With the help of fast autofocus, a sharp switch from an object to an object is possible, which will prevent the photo from getting blurry.

Of course, the disadvantages of full-frame cameras are also available:

  • Dimensions of cameras. Weight and size do not always allow you to easily transfer equipment, and without a tripod, your hands get tired quickly enough.
  • Slow shooting speed. Despite the fast autofocus and continuous shooting, instantly capture the moment still will not succeed.
  • The cost of cameras and additional equipment.
  • A careful approach to the technique and selection of optics. Many full-frame cameras do not accept lenses of a different brand.

As we can see, the number of pros and cons of full-frame technology is the same. So, everyone is free to choose, based on their tastes and preferences.

The differences between mirror and mirrorless

To begin with, it is worthwhile to understand the difference between SLR and mirrorless cameras. The main difference lies in the fact that the former have a mirror, while the latter do not, therefore, the mechanism of operation for such models will be different. Mirrorless cameras are a more budgetary and convenient option, respectively, the quality is a little worse, but good mirrorless ones can compete in quality with SLR cameras.

Now let's move on to full-frame cameras. Is there a difference between them in mirror and mirrorless models?

Since full-frame technology requires an additional purchase of optics, in the case of mirror models it will be possible to save. Here the rule works: buy one large body and many small lenses or one small camera and a considerable number of large lenses. This also applies to weight. For mirrorless cameras, you will also need to carry extra batteries with you.

As for built-in optics, mirrorless cameras also lose here, since not all lenses have, for example, IBIS, so it will be easier to find Nikon lenses for full-frame DSLRs, but you need to know that a non-native lens may not work.

If small-scale cameras do not have special differences in SLRs and mirrorless cameras, then in the case of full-frame cameras, it is better to give preference to mirror models, so as not to suffer with a lot of lenses and batteries.
