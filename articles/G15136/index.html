New Year in Tallinn. Winter holidays in Estonia

If you are tired of the traditional feast in front of the TV on New Year's Eve, and you want to experience new sensations from the most fun holiday of the year, we suggest you take a fascinating journey through the Baltic states. More precisely, go to Estonia, in its magnificent capital - ancient Tallinn.



This is one of the most beautiful cities in Europe. It was founded in 1154, and many unique buildings of those times have survived to the present day. New Year in Tallinn is a huge number of decorated Christmas trees, flickering garlands and candles burning in every window.

The daylight in December is about six hours, and the weather is pretty warm. Rarely does the temperature drop below two degrees of frost. In the afternoon, it is held at +2 degrees. The good news is that rains this month are a very rare occurrence. The weather is clear. And on New Year's Eve, snow may fall, which will turn the city into a fairy tale.

You will be greeted by orange tiled roofs with carved weathercocks, old streets with lanterns and centuries-old buildings softly wrapped in snow. The air flavors of marzipan, cinnamon and pine needles, mixing with the multilingual voice of many visitors. Tours to Tallinn for the New Year today are purchased not only by Russians, but also by Europeans.



If you stay in Estonia until mid-January, you may need an umbrella. This month, the average temperature is slightly below zero. It is much warmer during the day, cooler at night, but not lower than -5 degrees. Snow rarely falls in January, but it can rain. At this time, the streets of the city are covered with a thin crust of ice.

Things to do?

No one will be bored here, but you will have the opportunity to walk a lot (the weather in Tallinn has it), admire the local architecture, learn the interesting centuries-old history of the city, enjoy unusual dishes in restaurants and cafes, and learn ancient legends.



Numerous temples hold Christmas and New Year concerts and services. For example, in the Dome Cathedral you can get to the evening of organ music, and on the Town Hall Square to enjoy the performance of musicians and dancers.

What to see?

Tallinn is divided into two parts - Vyshgorod, which is called chivalrous, and Lower Old Town. In the old days, it was a shopping district. From Virus Gate, the Old Town begins. Previously, there were shops and workshops of artisans of the Dominican monastery.

Since 1441, the most important Christmas tree in Estonia has been decorated on Town Hall Square. Here every year a grandiose New Year and Christmas fair unfolds. There is also the oldest pharmacy, which appeared in the city in 1422, and in which today you can buy wine with various spices prepared according to an ancient recipe, as well as various sweets and marzipan. This pharmacy has a small museum.

Here you can see the most ancient clockwork, stained glass windows and city clocks of the XVII century. This street ends with the Tolstaya Margarita tower and the Sea Gate.





On the parallel Lai street there is an amazing building that belongs to the Middle Ages - "Three Sisters". Today it houses the luxurious Tallinn hotel. On the unusual street-stairs Luhike Jalg, you can approach the Niguliste Church, which houses the world-famous painting by B. Notke "Dance of Death".

If you like a more active holiday, visit the Singing Field. In winter, great slopes for snowboarders and skiers work here. By the way, many residents of Moscow and St. Petersburg come to Tallinn for the weekend and have a great time here.

Where to go with the kids?

Surely children and teenagers will be captivated by the numerous castles and museums in Tallinn. They will also like the zoo, which contains 350 species of animals.

In Tallinn, the Miia-Mila-Manda Museum for Children is dedicated to the nature of Estonia. It is designed for children up to 12 years old. For parents with very young children, the Wemb Tembumaa amusement park is suitable. You can watch a spectacular performance at the city Puppet Theater. Go to the Marzipan Alley, where children will be taught how to make funny figures, which you can then take with you.
