Ivan Fedorov: biography, years of life, photo

The first book printer in Russia bore the name Moskovitin. But to his descendants, he became known as Ivan Fedorov. The biography of this remarkable person is rich in events and travels, from which it is important to highlight the most significant details. These brief theses of the life of a great man became the basis for creating books on the topic "Ivan Fedorov, a biography for children." The biography of this person will be interesting to everyone who is interested in the development of Russian literature, primarily for young readers. The biography of Ivan Fedorov for children should indicate the main points of his activity as an associate and first printer. After all, the development of the Russian language is impossible to imagine without prints. And the name of the initiator of the Russian book is Ivan Fedorov.

short biography

The years of life of the first printer are 1510-1583. The exact date of birth of Ivan Moskovitin is unknown. His surname, most likely, did not come from a generic name, but from the place of birth. In those days, Russia was called a small principality, territorially assigned to the Commonwealth. The vast northern territories of modern Russia in the 16th century were known to foreigners as Muscovy.



It is known that at a young age, Ivan traveled a lot and studied at European universities. Literacy among Europeans struck Ivan Moskvitin - after all, by that time the printed book had been known in Europe for more than a century. The level of education was many times different from what Ivan Fedorov saw in his homeland. The biography would not be complete without the stories about how Europe impressed him.

First printing house

An interesting biography of Ivan Fedorov for children must necessarily indicate the place of the first printing house, which was located in our country. The first printing workshop was opened in Moscow.

Her activity is firmly connected with the name of its owner, who called himself Ivan Fedorov. A brief biography of this man indicates that he did not start this good work alone, but with a typographer and partner, whose name was Pyotr Timofeevich Mstislavtsev. According to the decree of Tsar Ivan the Terrible, books of religious content were to be published in the printing house. Ivan Fedorov was appointed responsible for the sovereign printing house. A brief biography for children may indicate that the first printer was a jack of all trades - he himself cut out complex engravings using pear wood boards for himself, invented a collection of fonts, and decorated his first books himself.

Watchmaker

The second book published in the Moscow printing workshop was The Watchmaker. Its authors were still Peter Mstislavets and Ivan Fedorov. The biography of the Russian book printer dwells little on his second book. It is known that it was also a religious publication, and was allowed to print with the full approval of the Orthodox Church.

Relocation

The biography of Ivan Fedorov for children should not be based on the sad episodes of his life. For a number of reasons beyond its control, the printing business in Moscow had to be curtailed. Perhaps the reason for their departure was the immediate danger posed by the new soldiers of Ivan the Terrible - the guardsmen. The first printers left the principality of Moscow and settled in the Grand Duchy of Lithuania, in the city of Zabluduow, which is currently located in Poland. The fame of the first printers reached these remote places - Fedorov and Mstislavets were warmly greeted in the courtyard of the hetman Grigory Alexandrovich Khotkevich. A great zealous of Orthodoxy and a supporter of the independence of the Principality of Lithuania, he offered the first printers his protection. Soon, under his patronage, a small printing workshop was founded in which books were published in Church Slavonic.



The Teaching Gospel

The first Zabludovskiy edition was the "Teaching Gospel", published in 1569. After his release, the paths of the first printers parted - Mstislavets went to the city of Vilno, and Ivan Fedorov took care of the fate of the printing house. The biography of that period of life shows that the case was put on a firm basis, and new books found their readers. It is important to know that in those days books were not only a source of knowledge, but a means of investing capital. Printed materials were very expensive, and enterprising rich people preferred to invest in books, not caring about what was written in them. Be that as it may, the Teaching Gospel showed the success of this undertaking, and Ivan Fedorov began to think over the publication of a new book.
