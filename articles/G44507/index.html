How to set up the antenna on the TV: step-by-step instructions, tips

The most obvious benefit of having a television antenna is the freedom from monthly cable TV charges. Despite the fact that cable TV provides many interesting channels, but still limits the choice of the user. The presence of its own antenna allows you to view more free broadcast channels - news, music, educational, children's and sports. A television antenna broadcasts your favorite channels, even if the satellite signal or cable is weak or unavailable.

Such antennas are especially useful in disadvantaged climatic regions where, for example, satellite and cable signals can be easily lost in cloudy weather, and you can enjoy your favorite shows with a digital television antenna, no matter what. The only obstacle to use is the complexity of installation and configuration. Therefore, before setting up the antenna on the TV, the user needs to get acquainted with all the "pitfalls" of the technology.

Parameters of high-quality signal transmission

A digital television signal arrives at a device with a finite amount of energy. In order to standardize and measure this indicator, the term ‚Äúnoise margin‚Äù is used, expressed in decibels (dB), it represents the amount of noise at which the signal will no longer be received. The ideal space does not exist today, the signal always encounters an obstacle on the way from the tower. Any interference between it and the receiving antenna is the noise subtracted from the power headroom. That is, it affects the quality of the "picture". The indicator must be taken into account before setting up the antenna on the TV.

If the noise margin is greater than 0, it can be adopted by the tuner to display the channel on the TV. At the same time, the line between the antenna and the TV tuner has its own noise, which is caused by the coaxial cable, signal splitters and the tuner that receives the signal. Any component between the antenna and the receiver can generate noise.

If the noise is too loud, this can lead to a negative performance and lack of reception. Before setting up the antenna on the TV, you need to decide how to position it correctly, which will require two important information. First, which channels have a positive noise margin in the location. secondly, in what direction the tower generates a signal.

Using the signal analysis tool from TVFool.com will help establish the noise margin (NM) and direction in degrees (‚ÄúMagn‚Äù in azimuth) to fine tune the antenna.

Internal reception of television antennas

There is a pie chart that shows the direction of movement of the television towers. You need to familiarize yourself with it before setting up the antenna on the TV. Many smartphones have a free application installed or available to determine it. If all the channels of interest are in the same direction, then you can install a directional antenna. A directional antenna receives signals from one direction, unlike an omnidirectional antenna, which can receive signals from several directions.

The advantage of directional is a higher "gain" of the television antenna than omnidirectional. This gain, also defined in dB, is added to the noise margin, counteracting some of its loss. Essentially, the larger the directional antenna, the higher the television gain.

An obvious disadvantage of a directional antenna is the need to reassign the antenna when switching the channel to the tower in the other direction. If after the antenna receives the signal, there is sufficient noise margin, examine the line to the TV. This signal loss occurs due to splitters, coaxial cord and TV tuner and others. The standard RG-6 coaxial cable has a loss of 5.65 dB per 100 feet of cable.

Television amplifier

There is a loss of noise margin associated with the amplifier. Since these losses are less than the losses generated by the TV tuner, in many cases it makes sense to use an amplifier before setting up the TV on a digital antenna.

For example, a TV tuner with a loss of 8 dB and a two-way splitter can lead to a loss of 11 dB (8 + 3 dB for a splitter). If you add a 2.9 dB loss for the proposed 50-foot coaxial cable, and then another 1 dB loss due to the connection at the end of the reception, the total loss is up to 14 dB.

If a 15 dB amplifier is added to the line immediately after the antenna, then the loss will be 0.0 dB. The amplifier also carries noise, for example, 3 dB will bring the total noise loss in the line to 2.0 dB.

If you add a 20 dB gain amplifier with the same noise level, it will only guarantee an unhindered signal level from the antenna to the TV, in other words, the pre-amplifier will not help the television antenna pick up a signal that is already outside the interference. This circumstance must be taken into account. Only then do they learn how to tune the TV to a digital antenna.

There are situations when an amplifier is not needed or even harmful. If all the stations that need to be received fall on an antenna with a noise margin of less than 30 dB and only 3 dB is lost in the line, gain is not required. In addition, if the signal is excessively amplified, this may lead to the tuner not detecting the radio station at all.

Receiving device selection

In order to determine which digital antenna provides the best television signal reception, it is important to know the location, since the distance from the nearest transmitter will determine the type of antenna that you need.

There are three choices:

  1. Internal antennas if the viewer lives within a radius of 15 km from the nearest transmitter. An antenna of this type is easier to install, since no additional metal structures are needed. Before buying, check the specifications to clarify the reach of each internal antenna, since there are antennas with different range. State-of-the-art internal digital antennas provide the best possible reception thanks to their innovative technology, even in places with a weak signal.
  2. Outdoor antennas. If the viewer lives within a radius of 15-30 km from the nearest transmitter, then you should use an outdoor antenna for crystal clear reception. The most common Yagi models that are easy to recognize thanks to their traditional design. Modern outdoor devices are designed to work in the most severe weather conditions, which must be considered before setting up the TV through the antenna. Otherwise, you can make a mistake with the choice.
  3. Loft antennas are becoming increasingly popular, especially in new buildings, as most households do not want the antenna cluttering up a new modern home. Placing the antenna in the attic looks like a logical option, it does not interfere with the interior, but it is important to make sure that the antenna is located correctly and at the required height.

4G / LTE mobile phone networks partly use the same bandwidth as DVB-T, and therefore can interfere with signal clarity. One For All antennas have a built-in LTE / 4G filter to eliminate this interference, providing crystal clear reception. It is recommended that you check the specifications beforehand to see if the antenna has a built-in 4G / LTE filter.

Preparing to Connect

Before connecting, determine the type of antenna connector on the TV, which is usually located at the rear or side. There are two main versions of this input:

  1. Standard for most modern devices, a coaxial RF threaded cylinder with a hole in the center.
  2. Connection for old IEC televisions.

Before setting up the TV antenna on the TV, find out the location of the nearest radio station. There are special signal analysis sites such as AntennaWeb.org and TVFool.com. They can tell you exactly how strong a antenna is needed to perceive available signals based on a residential address. It is recommended that you select an HDTV antenna that is powerful enough to receive all signals. An overly powerful television antenna can suppress a digital tuner if the user is in a high signal area.
