Basket weaving. What is the name of this hobby, you know?



Early information about the existence of various containers in the form of baskets for storing grain dates back to the pre-Mongol period. As for our time, the handicraft industry for the manufacture of wickerwork was widespread in the everyday life of people who lived in the 19th and early 20th centuries. Basically they were baskets with one or two handles made of twigs, straw, bark, stems, roots, shingles and differed in shape. The purpose of these products was the most diverse, they were used to collect grain, mushrooms, nuts, berries, vegetables, as well as to store and carry household supplies and household items.

Basket weaving. What is the name of this type of decorative art?

Nowadays, decorative and applied art for the manufacture of wicker products from vines, the so-called wickerwork, is widespread. However, modern masters are not limited only to baskets, they skillfully perform other products. Wicker baskets made of twigs can be of any shape - round, square, rectangular, with an open top or closed, may have lids of different designs or not at all.

Basket weaving. What is the name of the master who creates wickerwork?

In Russia, there have always been many talents who perfectly mastered such art as basket weaving. What is the name of a person who is able to create beautiful work of authorship from the vine? Of course, the dowser. Experienced craftsmen are able to create almost any object from willow twigs - from hats and strollers for children to furniture and huge figures to decorate the garden.

Those who are interested in this forgotten craft will benefit from the advice of experienced craftsmen published in a book called Weaving from a Vine for Beginners. In order to begin to engage in weaving, you do not need to invest money or buy expensive equipment. Material for wickerwork can always be found in abundance along the banks of rivers and swamps, where willow grows. Nowadays, environmentally friendly products are highly valued, especially those that are made in a single copy by hand.

Wickerwork, like any applied art, requires bestowal and patience. Its undoubted advantage lies in the fact that everyone, even a completely blind person, can do it.
