Origin

The Celts - these are the Gauls in the broad sense of their ethnicity. Being part of the common pagan culture of Europe north of Rome, they possessed all the characteristics characteristic of this group. Gauls as a people formed in the 7th century BC. e. in the region between the Rhine and the Upper Danube. Gradually they moved west and settled in modern France.

In the IV century BC e. the Gauls took several campaigns against their neighbors. In 397 BC e. they attacked the Etruscans living on the banks of the Po River, and in 390 BC e. even attacked Rome, however, their attempt to capture the city ended in failure. Leader Brenn was the organizer and inspirer of this campaign in Italy.

Gradually, the Celts were driven out of Central Europe and Germany.





Romanization

Fatal for the Gauls was the defeat in the war against the Romans in 59-51. BC e. The army of the republic was commanded by Julius Caesar. The commander conquered all of Gaul and made it part of a single state. True, Caesar understood that it was not worth rushing to final conquest. Firstly, Gaul did not immediately become a Roman province. Secondly, about a third of the tribes received the status of allies of the republic or simply free people. The process of their romanization was gradual. In addition, the central government introduced relatively low taxes and military duties for the Gauls. This was done in order to appease the Celts and prevent an armed uprising. But they were still dissatisfied with Roman rule. In the 1st century, several uprisings broke out in Gaul. Gaul Slavery

Trying to answer the question of who the Gauls are, you need to look at the structure of their society. Like most peoples of antiquity, they were divided into free people and slaves. Their position was similar to that of the slaves of the Mediterranean. Today, most of the reliable material about the Gauls is contained in the notes of their conqueror Julius Caesar. It is significant that he hardly described the relations of free residents and slaves, since these orders were similar to the Roman ones.

When a noble man died, his slaves were burned on a fresh grave. There were relatively few slaves of this people, since they sacrificed most of their prisoners of war to pagan gods, namely they were the main group from which new slaves were taken.

The free population of Gaul was divided into three groups: druids (priests), nobles and commoners. Moreover, in the secular part of society there was no middle ground. The society was extremely layered in its welfare. The only advantage of the simple poor was its large number. Otherwise, their situation left much to be desired.

The people were ruled by a leader, as well as a council consisting of representatives of the most noble and influential families.





Army of the Gauls

The base of the Gallic army was cavalry. The Celts refused to use chariots. Serving in the cavalry was a prestigious affair; only privileged members of society got there. Trying to explain who the Gauls and Thracians, as well as other peoples of antiquity, historians often turn to the analysis of the army. She is a cast of society. The cavalry's favorite tactic was the swift attack of horsemen who rallied in close ranks. Enemies often could not stand such a friendly pressure and fled.

They also loved all kinds of beards and mustaches. Accessories like bracelets and beads were common. The Gauls' shoes were on wooden soles. The costumes were practical and durable, as these pagans skillfully mastered the craftsmanship of the production of fabrics.



Religion

Understanding the place of the supernatural in the life of pagans helps to realize who the Gauls are. The life and religion of this people were closely intertwined. A key part of the Gauls' faith was the cult of nature. Trees, forests, magic plants were worshiped. The Celts worshiped water - springs, rivers, lakes, streams and seas. The Gauls believed in the existence of healing springs and considered these places, hidden in deep forest thickets, sacred.

Knowledge of beliefs was transmitted among the Gauls through oral retelling. Therefore, after the death of civilization, there were no written sources about the religion of these pagans.





Druids

Firstly, their title was not inherited; they had to be earned by deep knowledge of their own faith. Secondly, the Druids were not only priests. They performed the functions of judges, healers and astronomers.

In their ritual rites, the priests used mistletoe. It was believed that this shrub has magical properties. Mistletoe was used in medicine, as well as for drawing lots and predicting the future.
