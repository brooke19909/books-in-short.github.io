James Watt - Inventor of the Steam Engine

James Watt was the one whose work culminated in the industrial revolution for England and the world. An engineer and inventor from Scotland was engaged in the improvement of the Newcomen machine, as a result of which he invented his universal engine.

early years





James Watt was born into the family of the ship builder and creator of various mechanisms, James. His mother, Agnes, was a representative of a wealthy family, at one time she received an excellent education.

The future inventor was born on January 19, 1736. The boy was born very painful, so he received primary education at home from his parents. The child could not play with peers due to poor health, so he spent most of his time on self-education.

As a teenager, his favorite subjects were astronomy and chemistry. He also liked to make models of mechanisms that his father created.

Upon reaching the age of a graduate of primary school, James entered the gymnasium. He showed great success in mathematics. The young man loved to read, and many of this he sought to test in practice.

At the age of eighteen, the young man lost his mother. This affected the health and affairs of his father, so James had to take care of himself on his own. The young man moved from Scotland to London for a year to learn a craft that was related to measuring instruments. Official training was supposed to take place for seven years, but James had only enough money for one year. He began his training with the manufacture of rulers and compasses. Soon, a young student could already make quadrants, geodolites and other complex instruments.

For this year, the young man almost did not go out. All the time he worked: in the mornings - for the owner, and in the evenings - on order. So he could feed himself. In addition, due to the fact that he was not considered an official student, they could forcefully take him to the navy on the street.

First work

After graduating, James Watt with poor health returned to Scotland. He decided to organize his own business in Glasgow, which consisted in the creation and repair of tools. But he had to face a union of artisans who forbade him to do this work. The reason was that James did not go through formal training. It did not even help that he was the only representative of his business in Scotland.





But the case is saving the young man. At this time, a batch of instruments for astronomy classes arrived at the University of Glasgow. They required constant attention, including installation. Through his friends, Watt gets the opportunity to work. He was appointed master of scientific instruments of the educational institution. He had the opportunity to create his own workshop.

In an educational institution, James meets Joseph Black, who studied chemistry. The master helps the scientist in the development of some chemical devices that advanced the chemist's further research.

Since 1759, Watt's affairs have improved. This was facilitated by a partnership with businessman John Craig. They organized the production of various tools and toys. The income of the inventor has increased significantly. Their partnership ended after six years due to the death of Craig.

After the success of the new machine, numerous attempts to fake it began. In the struggle for the reputation of their own business, Watt and Bolton were forced to spend considerable money on litigation. As a result, they were able to defend their rights.

The meaning of the invention

James Watt's engine patent was filed in 1769. The document determined that the author of the patent did not invent a new machine, but a steam engine. Watt did not fully understand how significant his future improvement would become.





The significance of the invention was that in the engine the piston moved under the influence of steam. Thanks to this, it was possible to repeatedly increase power, creating more pressure. There was no longer any need to increase the size. Thanks to the invention, it became possible to create a steam locomotive, and a little later, a steamer.

Recognition of merit

During the life of the inventor, the machine of James Watt revolutionized the industry. It is not surprising that he was elected a representative of many societies. They even wanted to award him the baronial title, but he refused it.

Societies in which Watt was elected:

  • Royal Society of Edinburgh.
  • Philosophical Society in Rotterdam.
  • Corresponding Member of the French Academy.
  • The Birmingham Lunar Society is an unofficial organization for scholars of the British Enlightenment.

Last years





James Watt's biography proves how versatile he was. The heterogeneity of his knowledge was genuinely surprised by the writer Walter Scott, who was personally acquainted with the inventor.

In the last years of his life, Watt was engaged in a machine of his own production, which could copy sculptural works, such as bas-reliefs, statues, vessels and so on.

The master died on 08/19/1819, in the eighty-third year of life. He was buried in Handsworth.

Family and Children

James Watt, whose inventions made a breakthrough in industry, was married twice. His first wife, Margaret Miller, died in 1772, giving birth to their fifth child. But only two children survived to adulthood, whose names, like their parents, were James and Margaret.

The second wife was Anne McGregor in 1777. Their joint children were named Gregory and Janet.
