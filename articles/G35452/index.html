German helmets of World War II: history and description

Even non-military helmets often feature swastikas to demonstrate support for the Nazi party. German helmets of the Second World War have great similarities, and this is important to consider when trying to determine the type of model. Small changes may be the only difference between one type of helmet and another. Knowing the details that make up the German helmet helps you figure out which model you have on hand.

Short story

During the 1920s and 30s, the German government began redesigning the steel German helmets of World War I in an attempt to improve their appearance and function. The vast majority of steel helmet models were destroyed in accordance with the terms of the Treaty of Versailles. Some helmets remained in active service to meet Germany's small military needs. As a result, a significant deficit was formed.

The M1917 was rebuilt and introduced as a specially patented ‚Äútransitional" model for parades and general use. The remaining stocks of wartime (M1916, M1917, M1918) were restored for the military and police. They were used before the National Socialists came to power in 1933.

In 1935, the military approved the new Stahlhelm combat helmet , known as the M1935. It resembled the M1917, but was lighter, more functional, and also significantly updated.





The geometric dimensions of the German helmet of the Second World War decreased, compared with the models of the First World War. The helmet has become more compact. The M1935 helmet evolved several times during WWII. Each change led to several newer variations, although each model had the same basic design. At least three models were made exclusively for combat: M1935, M1940, M1942. All three versions of this helmet were worn during World War II.

Stahlhelm translates from German as "steel helmet". The imperial German army began to replace the traditional Pickelhaube (needle combat helmet) with a Stahlhelm during the First World War in 1916. The original color is dark blue.





In 1934, tests began to improve Stahlhelm, the design of which was a development based on models of the First World War. Eisenh√ºttenwerke has been designing and testing prototypes.

The new German helmets of World War II were pressed from molybdenum steel sheets in several stages. The visor was reduced in size and the large protruding ears for the obsolete armor shield were removed. The ventilation holes were retained, but small hollow rivets were added.

The edges of the sheath were moved to create a smooth edge along the helmet. And finally, a completely new leather liner was made, which greatly improved helmet safety, adjustment and comfort. These enhancements have made the new M1935 helmet easier. It has become more compact and comfortable than previous designs.
