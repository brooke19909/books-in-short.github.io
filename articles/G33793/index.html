A little bit about the history of the holiday

How and where Fool's Day appeared is not known for certain. According to the most common version, it arose in ancient Rome, where it was called the festival of fools. In Russia, April Fools Day became known under Peter the Great through foreign ambassadors who played out each other and courtiers. As you know, the Russian Tsar reformer was very fond of jokes, so every year he invented something unusual, which surprised his friends and foreigners.

Before the advent of the print press, raffles were not widespread. That all changed when journalists started specifically launching April Fools newspaper ducks, which caused a real commotion. Later, television joined the rallies. For example, in 1957, the British were informed of an unprecedented pasta harvest in Switzerland, and they also showed a video report from the fields where the pasta was tied in sheaves. Oddly enough, it turned out a lot of those who believed this information and even ran shopping to look for seedlings of this "plant". Another famous April Fools' Day joke was born in Paris, when in the late 1980s one of the French publications published an article about the plan for dismantling the Eiffel Tower and its transfer to Disneyland. This information provoked a protest among the residents of the French capital, who did not even suspect that they were being played.
