Goddess Io: myths and legends of ancient Greece, images

It is known that ancient Greek myths were often built on plots drawn from real life, and the authors endowed fictional characters with their own features. That is why many ancient deities are far from examples of morality in their modern sense. An example of this is the story of the supreme thunderer Zeus and the young goddess Io.

The young mistress of the master of Olympus

The goddess Io, who came to the modern world from Ancient Greece, was of a very foggy origin. According to some sources, she was the daughter of the river god Inach, according to others - one elderly, but very loving king. Other options are also provided. However, this is an everyday matter, because it is known that even the mother of a child can not always confidently name the name of his father.

One way or another, but the goddess Io spent her adolescence in the temple of Hera, the all-powerful patroness of the marriage, who inadvertently took her to the staff of her priestesses. The young girl acted quite decently until she fell in love with her husband - the supreme god and master of Olympus, Zeus, who impressed all the representatives of the weaker sex with his male beauty. He did not force himself to persuade for a long time, and a love affair ensued between them - one of those that are repeated in different versions from the time of the universe.

The Beast Struck by Hermes

To top it off, Hera assigned an all-seeing guard to her captive - the multi-eyed giant Argus, who constantly harassed the poor thing with empty nit-picking. Perhaps the story of the goddess Io would have ended on this, if not for the conscience awakened in the soul of her former lover.

Hera's Revenge

Upon learning of the incident, Hera came in indescribable anger. First of all, she cast a spell on the fugitive, by virtue of which she was doomed to remain forever in the guise of a cow. In addition, by the power of magic she created a terrible gadfly - a giant insect that was supposed to everywhere pursue the goddess Io and, mercilessly stinging, inflicting unbearable torment on her.

A vainly bitten cow ran from an evil gadfly. She did not find salvation either in the ancient city of Dodon, famous for an exquisite temple, once erected in honor of the culprit of her troubles - Zeus, nor in the vastness of Asia, where she vainly dreamed of finding peace, neither on the shores of the seas, nor in the river valleys. Everywhere a vile insect from the family of "parasitic dipterans" (as is commonly expressed in the scientific world) pursued its prey.

A ray of hope glinting in the ice of Scythia

Only within the distant northern country of Scythia did a ray of hope awaken for the order of the already desperate goddess Io. An ancient legend tells that at the time when it reached the polar latitudes, there, its fellow countryman Prometheus was chained to one of the rocks - a mighty titan who gave people fire and doomed to suffer from day and night the eagle tormented his chest. Realizing as no one else the problems of his compatriot, he comforted her with the prediction that deliverance from troubles awaited her on the banks of the Nile.

Hearing this good news, Io hurried to Egypt, and in the wake of her flew a pretty chilled and covered with hoarfrost gadfly. From the frost, he became even angrier and rushed to the runaway like a rabid dog. The drafters of the legend are silent about how much and what torment she underwent along the way, allowing the readers themselves to imagine it. However, with all certainty, it is reported that on the banks of the great African river, the romance between the goddess Io and Zeus received an unexpected and happy continuation.

The fruit of love, ripened on the banks of the Nile

After longing for his former passion, the thunderer pretty strained and by the power of witchcraft managed to break the spell that entangled her insidious Hera. The vile gadfly has died out, and the cow's skin, which hid the delicate girl's skin for so long, suddenly melted and revealed to the world the old Io, shining with its unearthly beauty.

Zeus, languishing without a woman's affection (the spouse was in no hurry to return his former favor to him), hastened to wrap her in her arms - so hot and passionate that after a certain period she gave him his son Epaphus. To this fruit of love that broke out between the goddess Io and Zeus, the myths of Ancient Greece attribute the honor of being the first king of Egypt. He, according to the generally accepted version, is the ancestor of the mighty and glorious tribe of heroes, the legendary Hercules became the most famous representative of it.
