Maslow Pyramid: Analysis of Need Hierarchy Exceptions



The main essence of Maslow's concept is a hierarchy of human needs, the satisfaction of which occurs gradually: from the lowest to the highest. Only by satisfying basic physiological needs does an individual proceed to satisfy higher needs - in recognition and self-actualization. The standard Maslow pyramid has five levels:

1. Physiological needs. They are basic, because human life would be impossible without food. A hungry individual cannot think of anything other than food. From a very early age, the satisfaction of this need is trained and improved, and acquires a cultural setting.

2. Security needs. The first year of a child‚Äôs life is associated with a feeling of reliability, security on the part of parents, freedom from fear and anxiety. Getting older, a person expects safety within educational institutions from people who organize training and educational processes.

3. The need for love. Otherwise, you can call it a desire to be accepted. It occurs when the previous two are satisfied. Initially, a person begins to communicate with close relatives, and then awaits acceptance among peers, in the training and working groups.

4. The need for recognition. It can be achieved through a desire for achievement, which gives a person self-confidence, testifies to his competence in a particular field of knowledge. Recognition can be achieved through the establishment of a reputation in society, through self-affirmation and gaining high status.

5. The need for self-actualization. It is realized through creative free activity of a person, which helps to reveal his spiritual potential to him, as well as the desire for self-embodiment, for the realization of the potential of inherent opportunities and abilities.

    The Maslow pyramid can be fully realized in a person or needs are fixed at a certain level, directing an individual's activity to satisfy only the first two, three or four levels.
