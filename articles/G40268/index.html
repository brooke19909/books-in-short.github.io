Kathmandu - the capital of which state? Attractions Kathmandu

Nepal is an exotic country that invariably attracts tourists from all over the world. The capital , Kathmandu, is not only the largest, but also the richest city with attractions. The valley in which it is located is located at an altitude of 1360 m above sea level, and impressive Tibetan ridges rise around. Like India, geographically close, Kathmandu abounds in the temples of Hinduism. According to local legend, there are 10 million spirits worshiped by adherents of this polytheistic cult. With numerous idols, sculptures of a philosophically contemplative Buddha peacefully coexist. The Baghmati River, belonging to the basin of the sacred Ganges, cuts the intermountain basin of the Himalayas. On its shores and built over 2000 years ago, the capital of Nepal.

If we were asked in Mayak-style rhymed quiz question: "Kathmandu - the capital of which state is meant?" - not everyone, without hesitation, could give the right answer to it. Vague notions of the administrative division of Indochina and the territories northwest of it give rise to the most unexpected answers. No less tricky would be the question: "What is in the Kathmandu Valley - the capital of which state and what is its name?" Meanwhile, on the territory of this Himalayan intermountain there are seven objects of world cultural value included in the UNESCO list.

What is worth seeing?

When planning a trip in advance, Kathmandu should be combined into a single excursion route with the nearby cities of Lalitpur and Bhatakpur, which was the capital until the 15th century. UNESCO perceives them as a single cultural complex of the Kathmandu Valley.



In Russian fairy tales, the stupa is the permanent affiliation of Baba Yaga (whose name, by the way, is etymologically close to the name of the scroll of the Yajur Veda, a book about sacrifices, and can be translated as "woman priestess"). In Nepal, grandiose stupas are objects of worship. Bodnath, a semicircular titanic structure with the enlightened eyes depicted on it, watching the world, is the center of Tibetan Buddhism. According to legend, the ashes of the unknown Buddha, the predecessor of the founder and distributor of the philosophical trend of Prince Gautama, rest here.

The ancient rulers of Nepal found their last refuge in the palace square of Durbar in the center of the capital. Entrance to ancient tombs, a palace decorated with elaborate wooden carvings, is paid for foreigners. Hanuman, seated at the Golden Gate, honored by Hindus, a flying monkey, is watching for proper recreation. Krishna and Kumari spread their temples here.

Drowning in green Tundikkhel - the area for various festivals and local parades. People come here for picnics to relax with children. There are golden fountains and a sixty-meter tower of Dharahara with an observation deck.





Exotic attractions of Kathmandu are the safari park located in its vicinity, where the ruling royal dynasty hunts, and the exposition of the National Museum, Museum of Natural History. The park offers tourists elephant rides and other unusual forms of entertainment. The museum halls introduce archaeological finds, historical collections of national weapons, and vivid representatives of the flora and fauna characteristic of various parts of the country.

In the Kathmandu Valley

On the other side of the Baghmati River is Lalitpur. This name translates as "city of beauty" (it was originally named Patan). The main attractions here are four stupas, dotted with inscriptions and the temple of the Thousand Buddhas. The Royal Palace on Durbar Square unites ten courtyards delimited by walls. The city is simply flooded with masterpieces of nevar architects and stone carvers. Here is the winter residence of Shiva - the Kumbeshwar temple (in hot, humid summers, according to popular belief, Shiva is sitting on Mount Kailash in Tibet).

Mahabudda, a terracotta-tiled structure, in fact, does not represent 1000, but as many as nine-odd thousand more Buddhas. Each handmade clay tablet bears the image of one enlightened one. The patron saint of Lalitpur, who brings abundance and rain to the earth, is revered by both Buddhists and Hindus. The building in his honor is called Matsyendranath, from here in April a festival of idols is transported around the city in a chariot. In June, he also leaves for the summer residence, the temple of the neighboring village of Bungamati. Apparently, the idols of Lalitpur love seasonal travel.

The sights of Kathmandu are primarily of cult significance. The name of the ancient capital Bhaktapur in translation means "the city of believers." It is located in the foothills of Tibet on the banks of the Hanumanta River. The settlement was almost wiped off the face of the earthquake by the strongest earthquake of 1934, all the destroyed monuments of antiquity were subsequently carefully restored. The natural disaster, however, did not destroy the measured, centuries-old feudal way of life of local residents, which is worth looking at first. The earthquake and Nyatapola Temple spared in such a way that creates an optical illusion of the building soaring in the air.

In the valley, in addition to the mentioned Buddhist complex Bodnath, there is its "brother" Swayambhunath, as well as the majestic Hindu temples Pashupatinath and Changu Narayan.

Local holidays

Dates of celebrated events are regulated here by astrologers. The Hindu festival of Diwali lights is celebrated on the darkest day of autumn and is considered the beginning of a local new year. The colorful religious mysteries of Indra Jatra with sacrifices on the last day take place on Palace Square in the rainy season. The military parade and the bloody idolatry with the slaughter of animals mark the action of Dugra Puja, called upon by mystical magic to protect residents from accidents related to vehicles, the use of weapons in the coming year. Relatively quietly, the sacred thread feast takes place, associated with the ritual immersion of the Indians in the sacred waters of rivers and the manual spinning of a sacred cotton string.

A mysterious rite of its kind - the choice of the Nepalese "living goddess" Kumari, the embodiment of the spirit of Taleju. For these purposes, on the Central Square, the elders determine the "divinity" of candidates three to five years old, belonging to the Buddhist family of Shakya. Translated, "Kumari" means "virgin", all thirty-two girls strictly selected from hundreds of applicants are doomed to celibacy in the future. The little ones are placed in a room filled with the severed heads of buffaloes, around them begins the demonic dance of men in masks of unclean spirits. Those girls who are least likely to show their fear, and become doomed to bear the title of goddess. Perhaps it is impossible to recall, except for Kathmandu, the capital of which state is systematically, with universal popular approval, inflicting such horror on its own young children.

With all the abundance of golden statues, balconies, spiers, colorfulness, skillful architectural performance of mystical structures and sacrificial content of local cults, it can leave a somewhat painful impression. Therefore, it is worthwhile to think in advance about the advisability of contemplating Nepalese rites, choosing between the tourist's keen curiosity and peace of mind.
