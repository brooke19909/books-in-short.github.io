Olympic motto: "Faster, Higher, Stronger!" History of the Olympic motto

For the first time, the Olympics gathered its spectators in 776 BC. e. Competitions athletes took place near Olympia on the island of Peloponnese. An interesting fact is that during the games absolutely all the wars in the region stopped. Ancient Greece for over a millennium watched this unique sporting spectacle. The participants were warriors, men competing in running at a distance of about 192 meters (one stage) completely naked. Because of this, women were not allowed into the stands, and they did not take part in competitions.

The duration of the competition was only one day. Gradually, the program of the Olympiad expanded. First, two-stage running was added, then endurance running, pentathlon competitions, chariot running, pankration, fist fighting and others. The decision to increase the duration of the games to five days was made in the fifth century BC. e. The 394 year was unlucky for the Olympians, the competitions were abolished due to incompatibility with Christianity. After all, initially they were dedicated to Zeus and other gods from the sacred mountain. It was possible to revive the games only in 1896 with the efforts and efforts of Pierre de Coubertin in a symbolic place - in Athens. And since 1924, the Winter Olympics started.





Symbols of the Olympics

Games have their own symbolism - fire, flag, anthem, motto, rings and oath.

The fire came to the Olympic tradition from Greece: it was transferred from the Hestia altar to the sacrificial altar of Zeus during the games.

The flag of the Olympic Games in white without any border or frame with the image of five rings. White color represents the unity of all peoples, the world, and the rings on it represent the universal Olympic idea.

The anthem is performed during the raising and lowering of the flag, as well as on other solemn moments.

Pierre de Coubertin really liked the Latin saying and when, in 1894, when the IOC (International Olympic Committee) was created, the question arose which Olympic motto would be officially approved, de Coubertin did not hesitate and suggested "Faster, Higher, Stronger." The motto was presented to the public during the VIII Summer Games in Paris only in 1924.





The unofficial principle of Olympic competitions

De Coubertin is also credited with creating the unofficial motto of the Olympics, which reads "The main thing is not victory, but participation." In fact, these words were uttered by a bishop from Pennsylvania in 1908 during the London Olympics. Participation was understood as empathy for an athlete who failed to win, but fought to the last with all his might. Words were sent to Pietri Dorando, an Italian runner. On the eve of Dorando, he was disqualified due to outside help at the finish line, which he did not ask for when he ran the marathon. During the award ceremony, he still received a gold cup from a member of the royal family for his outstanding sporting achievement.
