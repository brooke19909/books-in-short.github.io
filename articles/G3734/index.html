Do you have tax ID tax debts? There is a convenient service for payers from FSN

Tax debts per se are a negative phenomenon in the business activities of any payer. Firstly, tax arrears of taxpayer identification numbers once again attract the attention of regulatory authorities to a business entity. Secondly, the entrepreneur incurs additional costs associated with the obligation to pay a fine in the amount of the debt. Thirdly, you still have to pay taxes.

Service FSN





Tax TIN tax debts can be checked at any time on the tax service website . A special service allows you to see such information both by tax number and name. However, the TIN continues to be the main identifier, since the surname, first name and patronymic can still coincide with several people, and the number is unique.

So, tax arrears under TIN can be checked in two ways. The first is to contact the district tax office, in which the entrepreneur is registered. Here, the inspector will announce the amount of the debt and issue a payment for its repayment. The second option is the most convenient for business entities - to check the tax debt under the TIN on the website of the Federal Tax Service.

Fraudsters go out

When typing the phrase "tax debt by taxpayer identification number" in any Internet search engine, you can see a large number of sites that provide such a service. Some of them offer to view the specified information for a symbolic amount. If money is required from you for providing such a service, then in this case we are talking about scammers who create such sites. Just payers need to know that this information is provided free of charge.

There are other sites on the Internet that allow you to find out tax debt without a TIN. However, using only information about the name, you can see not your information, but someone else's, because there are namesakes, both middle names and names can coincide.





And one more type of fraudsters was discovered - sites were created that allow you to find out what tax tax is for the TIN by entering your data, after such an introduction, the payer will allegedly then be sent the result to the specified e-mail. Such sources of information cannot be trusted, since on the website of the Federal Tax Service data are provided on-line, that is, immediately.

An example of such fraud is information about the allegedly provided services for paying traffic fines via the Internet. A sufficient number of citizens fell for this "bait" and made electronic transfers, despite the strange name of this Internet resource.
