History of Piccadilly Circus in London

She appeared in 1819. Designed to connect the street of the same name with Regent Street. Initially, the square was round. But later, in the 80s of the XIX century, a street was attached to it, as a result of which the geometry was disturbed.

The sculpture of Eros on Piccadilly Circus in London (England) appeared in 1893. An angel with a bow was made in honor of Shaftesbari - a philanthropist, a public figure who fought for the rights of children. True, the sculptor Alfred Gilbert did not get Eros, but Anteros, personifying a deliberate, mature love. He is the opposite of the frivolous mythical creature, which the ancient people represented with a magic bow in their hands. In 1928, the metro station in Piccadilly Circus was reconstructed. Huge billboards appeared here at the beginning of the 20th century. By the way, today a giant neon sign is one of the attractions of Piccadilly Circus.

In the 60s of the XX century, they wanted to reconstruct this part of the central district of London. They planned to make the area two-story. The project caused a stir in the English capital, but was never implemented. In the 80s, pedestrian paths were organized here. The famous fountain was moved a few meters to the side. It was not accidentally transferred. Tourists, once in Piccadilly Circus, primarily sought to take a closer look at the famous London landmark, as a result of which they risked falling under the wheels of a car.

In the southern part of the square is the Criterion Theater, built in 1874. It is located underground, designed for six hundred spectators. In the north-eastern part is the London Pavilion. Initially, it was in another building, demolished in the 80s of the XX century. Once in the London Pavilion there was a concert hall, a cinema, a shopping arcade. Now it has a different name - London-Trocadero.

In order to get to the London Pavilion, you need to go to the Piccadilly Circus Station and go through the crosswalk. On the third floor is the world famous Guinness World Records Museum. Three more interesting sights located on Piccadilly Circus: the Swan and Edgar buildings and the County Fire Office, as well as the Amurs of London Museum. It is worth telling more about them.

Swan and Edgar

This building is an important part of the architectural ensemble of the square. It was erected by John Nash at the beginning of the 19th century, but during this time, of course, it was restored more than once. Once upon a time, in Swan and Edgar there was a large store of the famous British company Swan & Edgar Limited, which ceased to exist in the late 1970s.

At the beginning of the 20th century, the building was badly damaged. In 1911, a rally of suffragists took place on the square. Four years later, during the First World War, Swan and Edgar suffered from a raid by an enemy airship. Later the building was rebuilt, the architect R. Blomfield gave it the characteristic features of the Baroque, as a result of which Swan and Edgar almost completely lost its original appearance. Today the building is owned by Virgin Megastores.

County Fire Office

The building was built at the beginning of the XIX century according to the design of Robert Abraham. For some time, the County Fire Office was used by one of the British insurance companies as its headquarters. The southeast side of the building looks at the legendary Pall Mall. Its first floor is an open arcade of five arches. From here you can go down to the Piccadilly metro station. Initially, the facade of the building was decorated with columns of the Corinthian order. Today he is crowned with a clock, a dome, two chimneys. The County Fire Office is decorated with a sculpture "Britain", depicting a woman sitting on a lion, in a helmet, with a trident and a shield.
