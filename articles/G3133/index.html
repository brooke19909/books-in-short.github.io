He is one of those people who literally managed to push the historical process in a certain direction. Of course, the last statement has nothing to do with the moral side of his philosophy and activity.

Adolf Hitler: the path to power

After the First World War, being extremely disappointed with the surrender of Germany, the young corporal again returned to his casual earnings, but already in Munich. An accident turned his fate here abruptly. By the will of fate, he was destined to be in one of the beer establishments of the city, where the local patriotic party (then called the Workers' Party of Germany) held its meeting at the same time. A guy who was keen on politics was interested in their ideas, and in 1920 he joined this still small society. And soon, thanks to his own charisma and punchy perseverance, he became her most important person. Hitler's first attempt to come to power dates back to 1923. We are talking about the famous November Beer putsch, which ended in failure. As a convoy of coupists marched through the streets of Munich, they were stopped by police who opened fire on the rebels. An interesting story from the recollections of eyewitnesses is conveyed by a famous researcher (and a former journalist in Weimar and Nazi Germany) William Shearer: under the barrage of fire, the putschists were forced to lie down on the ground; immediately after the police stopped shooting, the party leader first jumped up and started to flee from the scene of the collision, then got into the car and drove off. Strange, but the flight of Adolf Hitler did not affect his authority. Moreover, coping with the first fear, he very boldly behaved on

the ensuing lawsuit, which even added to his sympathy. However, for the attempted coup, the young politician was nevertheless sent to prison in the Landsberg fortress. True, he spent less than a year there.

Adolf Hitler: a political biography

And having been released at the end of 1925, he again began his struggle for power. With fiery speeches, cunning political actions, outright blackmail of other political forces, violent reprisals against his opponents and outright deceit in Nazi propaganda, the NSDAP only a few years later became the country's most influential force. And in 1933, Adolf Hitler forced the then President of the Republic, Paul von Hindenburg, to make himself Chancellor. From this moment on, the NSDAP is rapidly becoming a united political force in the state, their ideology is the only true one, and Germany is plunging into a totalitarian regime.
