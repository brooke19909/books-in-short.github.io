What is genocide? The meaning of the word "genocide". Genocide of peoples in history

Sometimes this term, which has a sharply negative meaning for the entire civilized world, is confused with bursts of social aggression similar in nature. In this article we will consider what it really means, we will highlight its most vivid manifestations in its monstrosity.

Definition

So, genocide is a crime committed to destroy, to undermine as much as possible a certain group of people on the basis of:

  • Aggressive beliefs that some human races prevail over others. Attempts to eradicate those who are not similar on biological grounds.

Genocide is a phenomenon that, in addition to direct physical destruction, practices the creation of unbearable conditions in which the further development of the "enemy" is impossible.

For example, when it comes to religion, the practice of forcibly removing children from families. The widespread prevention of conception and procreation in some cases is also practiced.

History of the term

The official beginning of the recognition of the genocide was laid at the end of World War II. It was introduced by lawyer Rafael Lemkin, a citizen of Poland and a Jew by birth.

Members of his family became victims of the Holocaust, and it was with the term ‚Äúgenocide‚Äù that Professor Lemkin wanted to fully describe the monstrous atrocities of Nazi policies that killed many people not only from 1939 to 1945, but also the events that happened two decades before Togo. The term "genocide" itself is based on the Greek word "genos", which means "genus", and the Latin "tsido", which means "kill".

Official recognition

In official documents, the word first appeared during the Nuremberg trials - the genocide of the people was included in the verdict, trying to more fully describe all the atrocities that the Nazis committed during the war.

However, this was not enough for the term to have legal force.

At the end of 1948, the UN adopted the Convention on Genocide Crimes. It most fully set out all the provisions that countries that accepted the Convention should have strictly adhered to. Genocide, regardless of its form and manifestation, must be prevented and severely punished. The only thing among the groups of people that could become potentially oppressed was that there was no place for those who were united by common political views. Armenian Genocide

At the end of April each year, the world recalls countless representatives of the Armenian people who fell victims of the Ottoman regime . The Armenian Genocide is a monstrous crime against humanity. From April 24 to the end of June, at least 1.5 million representatives of the Armenian intelligentsia were massacred in the Ottoman Empire. As a result, not a single native was left in the west of Armenia.



Before the outbreak of World War I , there were at least 4 million Armenians all over the world according to official figures, most of whom lived in the ill-fated Ottoman Empire. The ideology of the state, which is now called Turkey, did not tolerate representatives of non-Turkic peoples.

The Armenian genocide is the first open act of aggression that opened the way for others in the 20th century. It took place in 2 stages:

Kurdish genocide

Kurdish genocide is an act of aggression by the Iraqi government and with the blessing of its leader Saddam Hussein towards Kurdish tribal peoples. He went through several stages:

  • The first phase was carried out in mid-1983, when all men and boys over 15 years old were killed. All deported Kurds that belonged to the Barzan tribe were taken out of the camp in an unknown direction, no one returned.
  • The second stage of the scheme was similar to the first, but had a wider radius of destruction. That was Operation Anfal (Trophy), carried out by the Iraqi army for 2 years, starting in 1987. About 2 hundred thousand representatives of Kurdish tribes were killed or disappeared.

The full extent of the atrocities became apparent after the overthrow of Hussein - both mass graves and concentration camps were discovered in which at least 700 thousand people were imprisoned, who lost their freedom, but still managed to survive the Kurdish genocide. What did this give to Hussein? A sense of personal omnipotence and impunity, perhaps, but after the overthrow, this was quickly refuted. Nevertheless, about a million people became refugees, having lost their homes, not counting the dead.
