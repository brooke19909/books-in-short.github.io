The most stable currency: a review of world currencies

In our difficult time, almost everyone thinks about where to invest their money most rationally. However, at the same time, not all people are aware that any contribution is a significant risk, especially when it comes to not quite stable currencies. In this article, we will consider what is the most stable currency in the world today, its features, and also will review other strong national monetary units of the planet, which can also aspire to lead in the future in the financial sector.

Absolute leader

When studying world currencies, it is worth paying close attention to a country called Switzerland. Yes, not every one of us will immediately find this little power on the map, but for many years this country has been confidently holding the palm in its economic and financial stability. In this regard, we can say with full confidence that the most stable currency in the world is the Swiss franc. This fact is largely explained by the wise macroeconomic policies of the state leadership, as well as the presence of bank secrecy, guaranteeing confidentiality to an individual, company, corporation.

In 2011, the franc had a real opportunity to grow against the euro due to the economic crisis in the EU, but the head of the National Bank of Switzerland made a seemingly unpopular decision for many people in the post-Soviet space - not to allow the franc to grow above 1.2 euros, i.e. keep your own currency from strengthening.

Of course, the fact that the strongest currency in the world does not allow its own sharp rise and devaluation is very important from the point of view of investors who can safely make their investments in francs or keep bank savings using these banknotes, without worrying about sharp jumps in the exchange rate in one direction or another.

Miracle from the Middle Kingdom

Of course, it cannot be stated unequivocally that the most stable currency is the Chinese yuan. Despite its authority, the Asian banknote is still not the undisputed leader. Nevertheless, this monetary unit confidently holds the second position in the conditional rating of the most powerful national currencies in the world.

The yuan began to gain strength after in 2005 it lost its peg to the US dollar. In the past few years, Chinese government leaders have embarked on a managed devaluation of their own currency in order to increase the economic growth of the state. At the same time, against the ruble, the yuan has risen in price by 228% over the past ten years.
