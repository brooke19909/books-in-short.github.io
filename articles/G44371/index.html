European Space Agency: creation history, functions and activities

ESA (European Space Agency) was established in 1975. As of today, it includes 22 countries. The main objective of the organization is the cooperation of its participants with each other and at the international level in the development and study of outer space for its peaceful use.



History of creation

The agency was formed on the basis of two European organizations through their merger. The first of them was engaged in the creation of launch vehicles, and the second in the development of satellites. ESA is headquartered in Paris. In addition to its permanent members, this includes several observer countries, including Canada, which takes part in some programs. On an ongoing basis, the agency has fourteen countries: France, Germany, Belgium, Austria, Great Britain, the Netherlands, Italy, Spain, Switzerland, Norway, Sweden, Finland, Ireland and Denmark.

Destination

The main mission of the organization is space research , development, launch and operation of automatic interplanetary stations, Spacelab laboratories, Hubble telescope and others. The Agency actively cooperates with the national space programs of the states participating in it. The largest countries that make up the organization oversee certain areas. Germany has been assigned the function of creating automatic cargo ships and personnel training centers for their maintenance. France is developing carrier rockets and satellites, which should greatly simplify space exploration, and is also responsible for the operation of the Kourou space center. Italy is developing interplanetary stations and modules for them.



Structural units

The ESA consists of five structural units. They are geographically dispersed throughout Europe. The first of these is the secretariat, whose headquarters are in the French capital. In the Dutch city of Noordwijk, the center for space technology and research is deployed, which is considered the main technical institution of the organization. It consists of numerous project teams, as well as a technology support department. There are also various test equipment related to such areas as space exploration. In Germany, two structural units are deployed at once. A space operations center is located in Darmstadt, which tunes satellites and ground-based equipment to communicate with them. An astronaut center is located in Porzvan, which specializes in training future cosmonauts and coordinating the activities of the entire European manned space program. A research institute is operating in the Italian city of Frascati, whose employees analyze and use data obtained from planetary observation systems from space.

Control

The European Space Agency is headed by a CEO and a board. They have the responsibility to fulfill all the tasks facing the organization. The main body is a council composed of representatives of all participating States. He approves all programs and activities of the organization, approves the budget and coordinates all financial issues. In addition, the Council approves or blocks the entry of new members into the European Space Agency. Each country has one vote here. All decisions are made based on the majority of votes. As regards financial matters, the support of 2/3 of the participants is necessary for their approval. The Council has several subsidiary bodies, which include committees responsible for administrative and financial policy, the implementation of scientific programs, international relations and industrial policy.



The General Director is the chief executive officer and legal representative of the agency. He subordinates all structural units of the organization. In addition, he represents her interests in NASA and other international organizations.

Activities

The European Space Agency cooperates with many organizations, as well as states that are not part of it. International activities are considered one of the key elements in ESA policies. In February 2003, an agreement was signed on cooperation between the organization and our country. Similar treaties apply to states such as Poland, Greece, Hungary, Portugal, Czech Republic and Romania. It should be noted that the activities of the agency are not limited only to Europe. In particular, for the quality use of satellites, fruitful relations have been established with Japan. The organization actively helps to develop space activities in other countries, for the representatives of which relevant courses are organized.



Among other things, the European Space Agency is actively collaborating with many international organizations. In particular, they are currently developing future meteorological programs, various explorations of outer space with a view to its further use for peaceful purposes, and training new personnel for these tasks.
