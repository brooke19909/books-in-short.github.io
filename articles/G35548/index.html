Favorite vacation spot of the Russian nobility





The professors' corner in Alushta in 2016 already collected positive reviews from most tourists who managed to visit it. This place has been popular among holidaymakers since the 19th century. For example, in 1886, Russian geologist Nikolai Golovkinsky settled here. After that, many other well-known and honored scientists began to build their summer cottages near his house, who were imbued with the beauty of the local places. Over time, many well-known academicians spent almost the entire summer here, for which reason these places were called the Professor‚Äôs Corner. Here was the maximum concentration of representatives of the Russian intelligentsia.

For example, after going on a well-deserved rest, this place for quiet old age was chosen by Nadezhda Suslova, who is considered the first female doctor in Russia. It was she who initiated the opening in Alushta of a school for village children, which the children could attend absolutely free.

Today, Suslova and Golovkinsky in the vicinity of these places are monuments. They are located in the village of Lazurny on the Black Sea coast.

Twentieth Century Professorship





In the XX century, the popularity of this place among the representatives of the Russian intelligentsia did not weaken. The life of the Russian writer Sergei Sergeev-Tsensky, the author of the novel "Babaev" and the epic "Sevastopol Strada" is closely connected with this resort.

In 1944, by decision of the city council of Alushta, a summer residence was provided to the writer. He settled in the house of academician of architecture Alexei Beketov, the author of masterpieces in the tradition of the Russian classical architectural school, who died three years earlier.

It is to the Crimean period of creativity that the novel of Sergeyev-Tsensky, "The Guns Talked," belongs. This work, on which he worked for several years, he managed to complete only on the Black Sea coast. Here Sergeyev-Tsensky wrote literary memoirs "My acquaintance with I. E. Repin." Today , a memorial plaque is installed on the house in which he lived and worked.
