What is shilling? The meaning of the word, history

What is shilling? This question was asked by anyone who at least once came across this term. This article will answer this question.

Shilling. Definition

Shilling is a generalized name for a number of metal bargaining coins of Western Europe. In the XX century, the national monetary units of some Western European countries also bore this name. It was from shilling that the name "shelag" came to the Old Russian language.



In some states, shilling is still used today, in particular in a number of African states that were previously colonially dependent on the British Empire.

History

In modern Germany, shilling began to be used in the XIV century. From the fifteenth century, it began to be used in the Danish Kingdom and the Netherlands, and in the sixteenth century, shilling came into circulation in England.
The English king Henry VII in 1502 ordered the minting of the first shilling in the British Isles. Initially, the coin was called "teston". Only under King Edward VI the coin acquired its current name. British shilling was used in the country until 1971.

In addition to the UK, shilling was used in Austria (replaced by the euro in 2002). Today, shilling is used as the official currency in a number of East African states, such as Kenya, Somalia, Tanzania and Uganda. They are also joined by the self-proclaimed state of Somaliland.

British shilling. Coins

British shilling is a coin that was used as bargaining chip in England. The people nicknamed him "Bob."



One British pound was divided into 20 shillings. In 1971, the shilling, whose photo you can see above, was replaced by pence. One shilling was equated to 5 pence.

The most common coins in England were two (florin) and five (krone) shillings. In addition to metal coins, paper notes with a face value of ten shillings were also issued.

Exchange operations. Collecting

All instances of West European shillings, which were relatively recently used in different countries of Europe, now represent only collection and cultural value. However, numismatists and bonists from around the world are happy to purchase shillings in their collection.

The cost of shillings in the collectors' market is formed due to many factors: the year of minting or printing, country of residence, face value, degree of preservation, mint, etc.



The situation with modern shillings, that is, African ones, is completely different. They are not just collectors who do not want to buy them, but even residents of those countries where they are in official circulation are not particularly keen to get their currency. They are much more attracted by the opportunity to receive foreign money: dollars, euros, British pounds, etc. This is due to the fact that local currency units are very cheap and constantly depreciating, so getting paid in national currency is not only unprofitable, but also risky. because at any time there may be a devaluation of the state currency.
