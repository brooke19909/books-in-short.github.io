Who invented the dollar: the history of occurrence, stages and evolution

The dollar is almost the most popular and coveted currency of the globe. Recently, the market is gradually capturing the euro, which claims to be world dominant. However, the old "green" bucks is not yet losing ground. Perhaps the one who invented the dollar did not count on such a stunning reputation of his brainchild.

The most famous currencies of the world

Before we look at a brief history of the emergence of the dollar, I would like to dwell a little on the money resources of the world as a whole. Today, almost all European countries that are members of the European Union are gradually switching to a common currency - the euro. But in many still along with generally accepted national banknotes.

The most famous currencies of the world are:

  • Euros are the common money of the Eurozone countries. Introduced into non-cash circulation on January 1, 1999, the currency from the beginning of 2002 began to be actively used by the population of the European Union.
  • US dollars, no matter what, are still competitive money.

  • The Japanese currency is one of the most popular in the international Forex market.
  • Although the range of use of the Swiss franc is small (Switzerland and Liechtenstein), this money is strong enough to participate in trading on the exchange.

A brief biography of the one who invented the US dollar

The name of the creator of the most popular currency in the world is Oliver Pollock. For a long time he and his family lived in Ireland, but by the will of fate was forced to emigrate to America. At first, Pollock settled in the English colony of Pennsylvania. However, he soon moved to Louisiana, New Orleans, where he developed his own business. Along with trading, he was engaged in the cultivation of indigo, tobacco and sugarcane.

During the American Revolution of 1775-1783, known as the United States War of Independence, Pollock bought weapons from the Spaniards and resold them to American patriots. He recorded all his transactions in books, where for the first time he used the dollar sign to indicate revenue.

Dollar: $ sign history

The American currency needed to be somehow designated so that it was easily recognizable among other money from other states. All the same, Oliver Pollock invented the dollar sign on April 1, 1778. The designation of the Spanish peseta, which was common in the United States of America at that time, served as a model for a kind of logo. Two lines on top of the letter S served as a symbol of the pillars of Hercules, supporting the emblem of Spain. Two powerful columns, braided with one tape, marked the edge of the land, and the slogan on the tape read: "Nec plus ultra" - "Nowhere to go."

Soon, in order to save time, the dollar symbol began to cross out with one vertical line. So the well-known sign of the American currency was born.

In addition to the official version of the appearance of the dollar sign, there are others:

  • According to the German version, the name of the money comes from the German "thaler", the reverse of which adorned the image of a snake entwining a cross. Subsequently, it was this pattern that served as the basis for the dollar sign.
  • The British theory says that the famous symbol came from the designation of the English shilling S, reinforced by a vertical line.
  • There is a Portuguese hypothesis regarding who invented the dollar icon. The S character is very similar to a comma, separating in numbers the tenths of the hundredths. Probably the one who invented the dollar sign, and did not suspect such a story.
  • The Roman version compares the dollar sign with the symbol of the ancient Roman sestercia coin - HS. In the American version, N overlapped S and was left without a vertical crossbar.
  • There is another curious version of the origin of the sign of the American currency. The so-called slave theory says that the vertical lines symbolize the pads in which the slaves were shackled, and the letter S is the bent figure of the servant.

Why is the green dollar

It is not known exactly who came up with the dollar design. Perhaps it was developed by a whole group of people. However, there are interesting facts explaining why the American currency is of such a color.

In 1869, an agreement was signed between the United States Department of the Treasury and the Philadelphia company Messers J. M. & Sox to produce special paper for watermarked money. Then the Treasury of the country began issuing the first dollars using green mascara. Thus, the one who invented the color of the dollar automatically became the guarantor of additional protection of the currency from fakes. With the advent of photography, old banknotes made in black were very easy to make using the photographic method. In addition, the use of a single color significantly saved waste.

Today, in the production of new banknotes, yellow and pink are added to the main green color.

What American Greens Are Made From

The first dollars were issued from special paper produced by only one firm. The company did not have the right to sell its products to anyone other than the federal authorities of the United States. By the way, the ink formula is classified information from the US Bureau of Engraving and Printing.

Modern bucks are produced by two factories - it is located in Texas Fort Worth, the second - in Washington. As a material, special paper is used, which has enhanced protection in the form of microprints and special threads. In addition, in order to avoid counterfeiting, it is supposed to change the appearance of banknotes every 7-10 years. Old bills are being taken out of use gradually.

The approximate weight of one bill is one gram. The composition of the paper is as follows: 25% - flax thread, 75% - cotton fibers. Today, silk amplifiers, known before the First World War, are replaced with synthetic threads. Banknotes are very hardy, durable and do not turn yellow over time. The approximate service life of the bill is from 22 to 60 months.

The exchange ability of the American currency

The Pollock case - the one who invented the dollar - continued in 1792. By law of 1792, bimetallism with free coinage of gold and silver was introduced in the United States. Since 1873, the gold dollar has become the monetary unit. Officially, the U.S. gold standard was set in 1900 with 1.50463 grams of pure gold per coin. In 1934, the dollar was devalued and depreciated by 40.94 percent, its gold content since January 31, 1934 - 0.888671 grams of pure gold.

The economic crisis after the First World War required an increase in the discount rate, which, however, did not help much. Until 1900, the dollar was freely exchanged for both gold and silver. However, since 1900 it was allowed to barter only for gold.

At present, gold dollar coins have been retired and replaced with banknotes.
