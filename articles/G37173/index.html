Leeds Castle in England: history, photos, how to get there?

In little England, there are surprisingly many castles. Many of them are poorly preserved, and some are completely destroyed. One of the well-preserved medieval buildings is Leeds Castle. About his story and how to get to him, read the article.

The early history of Leeds Castle

Its location is the lake islands by the Len River. It's just four miles from Maidstone, Kent. Today it is perhaps the most beautiful medieval castle in the world, rich in its history and events taking place around it.





The castle is named after its first owner, who was the lord Lead. At first (857) a small wooden fortress was built. In its place much later (1119) a stone castle was erected. Since 1278, the castle has been in the possession of Edward the first, who remodels it and gives it to his wife, Eleanor of Castile. Until the beginning of the fourteenth century (1318), the widowed queens of England owned the castle. He was the residence of Margaret of France, stepmother Edward II.

Queen's residence

The owner of the castle in 1321 was Lord Badlesmer. When he was not at home, Queen Isabella came to stay with her, who was denied hospitality. Her guards were killed and she returned home. King Edward II, the husband of Isabella, responded to the insult. He arrested the Budlesmear relatives and sent them to the fortress, and Leeds captured.





Queen Isabella received the castle as a gift from her husband. Since then, no one has captured him. For many centuries, the castle was the residence of the English queens. Leeds belonged to the crown for a long time, from 1278 to 1552. In the Middle Ages, it was a favorite resting place of the English kings and queens who often visited it.

The history of the castle in our time

Lady Bailey Leeds Castle, a photo of which is presented for viewing, was inherited from a wealthy uncle in 1926. The lady spent almost all her fortune on its reconstruction and interior design. She hired the best craftsmen to work. With their help, the internal appearance of the castle was transformed.





A great contribution to its restoration was made by the French architect Arman Rato, and later by Stefan Budin. Thanks to the heiress, paintings, tapestries, furniture, Chinese porcelain, and ceramics were collected. Reconstructed many rooms for various purposes.

Seminar room

Leeds Castle has many premises. One of them is Lady Bailey's former room. She used her as a boudoir. In 1960, the room was transferred to her own son to equip her bedroom. When the lady died (it was 1974), the room was converted into a hall in which seminars began to be held.





The first meetings in this hall were held in 1978, where the foreign ministers of countries such as the United States of America, Egypt and Israel concluded a series of agreements called Camp David. The seminar hall was sought after by Sinn Fein when, during the fourth year of the third millennium, peace negotiations were held there to address issues related to Northern Ireland.

Banquet Hall of King Henry the Eighth

Leeds Castle in England has a room named after the famous king. Henry was the owner of the castle. It was during this period that a bay window was installed, distinguished by luxury and beauty. Royal chambers

In this room, restorers tried to keep the interior the way it was when the castle was owned by the widow of King Henry the fifth, Catherine Valois (1420). The Queen's bed was magnificent and rarely used for its intended purpose.





Its impressive size and delicate silk drapes impressed the guests whom the queen received. Bedspreads and drapes were marked with the initials "H" and "C", denoting the names of the king and queen. A diamond-shaped coat of arms hung above the fireplace, meaning that the queen was a widow.

Gallery

Road to the castle

How to get from London to Leeds Castle by car? A wonderful medieval building is located from London in a southeast direction just 64 kilometers away. This is the neighborhood of Maidstone. You need to go along the routes B2163, M20.

How to get to Leeds Castle by train? Special routes have been developed for direct train connections between Victoria Station in London and the nearest station on the Birsted Railway, from where regular bus routes run to the castle itself. The journey takes one hour. You can go on a journey from another station - Waterloo. Trains stop very close to the castle, only twenty minutes away.
