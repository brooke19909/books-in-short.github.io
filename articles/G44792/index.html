Amino acids: biochemistry, classification

For the full activity of the human body, the performance of all functions, it is necessary to use foods enriched with proteins, fats, carbohydrates. Proteins and proteins are components of cells, so a person needs protein food. What are amino acids? The biochemistry of these compounds is an important issue that deserves detailed consideration and study.





Amino Acid Features

These compounds are necessary for the synthesis of protein molecules. In nature, there are more than one hundred and fifty different amino acids, but not all of them are vital to the human body. What kind of amino acids do we need? The biochemistry of 20 such compounds has been studied in detail by domestic and foreign scientists. It turned out that twelve of them are capable of being synthesized inside the human body, and only eight amino acids a person should receive with food.





Classification

Consider some amino acids. Biochemistry, the classification of these organic compounds involves the allocation of three main groups:

  • irreplaceable received with food. Such substances cannot be synthesized in the human body;
  • interchangeable, formed in the body, falling into it along with protein food;
  • conditionally interchangeable, produced from irreplaceable compounds.

Duality of properties

What features do amino acids have? The biochemistry of these compounds is explained by the presence of two functional groups in the molecules. These chemical compounds have a carboxyl (acid) COOH group and are also amines. Such structural features explain their chemical capabilities.

The similarity with organic and mineral acids is manifested in reactions with active metals, basic oxides, alkalis, salts of weak acids. In addition, amino acids are able to enter into chemical interaction with alcohols, forming esters. The presence of an amino group explains their interaction with acids via a donor-acceptor bond mechanism.





Classification and Nomenclature

Depending on the location of the carboxyl group, it is possible to divide these organic compounds into alpha, beta, amino acids. The numbering of the carbon atom in this case begins with the carbon following the acid group.

In organic chemistry, amino acids are distinguished by the number of functional groups: basic, neutral, acidic.

Depending on the nature of the hydrocarbon radical, it is customary to subdivide all amino acids into fatty (aliphatic), heterocyclic, aromatic, and also sulfur-containing compounds. An example of an aromatic amino acid is 2 aminobenzoic acid.

According to the systematic nomenclature, when the name of this class of organic compounds is named, the position of the amino group is indicated by a number, then the name of the carbon chain, which includes the carboxyl group, is added. The Greek alphabet is used if the amino acid is named according to the trivial nomenclature.
