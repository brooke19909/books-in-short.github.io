Plein air - what does this word mean?

Everyone who studied at an art school, attended drawing courses or photography, probably heard the word "plein air" more than once. What it is, and how it differs from ordinary fine art, teachers had to tell in detail. The same article is intended for those who are not familiar with this concept.

What is a plein air

Not all artists compose their paintings while sitting in a workshop. And not everyone has the opportunity to draw from photographs, especially since the camera cannot convey the volume and feeling of space, no matter how hard it tries. To solve this problem, there is an open air - drawing in nature. And the object of creativity can be anything: plants and landscape, panoramas of the city and individual buildings, portrait, sky and much more that can only come to mind.



Plein air for artists is another opportunity to test your strengths, feel the natural light and transfer it all to the canvas. The main thing is to learn how to draw quickly before the light changes. Often in the open air, etudes and sketches are created, and not full-fledged paintings. Photographers also work in nature. For many of them, plein air is the main direction in creativity.

How hard is it

To paint in the open air is much more difficult than in the studio. The weather can change at any moment - the wind blows away all art supplies, insects interfere, and the sun quickly changes its position in the sky, radically changing lighting. It would seem, why so tormented, because you can take a picture of the scene and paint everything at home. But not so simple. Any artist will answer that the pictures drawn from a flat photograph will look flat themselves. And the artist‚Äôs task is to create a sense of space. No matter what they say about the open air (that, for example, it is a waste of time, effort and nerves), no standing master will refuse the opportunity to create in nature.





History

They painted for a long time in nature, but the open air as a painting style appeared only at the beginning of the 19th century, thanks to the work of the British Richard Parks Bonington and John Constable. The original genre, which implies quick drawing in nature, appealed to many. He was picked up by such famous artists as Pierre-Auguste Renoir, Baptiste Camille Corot and Jean-Francois Millet. Later, one of the most famous artists who painted nature from nature, became Claude Monet.

Plein air was especially popular in Russia from the end of the 19th to the beginning of the 20th century. K. Korovin, I. Grabar and V. Serov regularly worked in nature. Many stunning sketches in this genre were created by the famous landscape master Isaac Levitan.

At present, drawing in nature is often included in the compulsory program of art higher and secondary educational institutions. And professional painters arrange an international plein air. This is a group trip to nature, designed to exchange experience and knowledge between artists from different countries.
