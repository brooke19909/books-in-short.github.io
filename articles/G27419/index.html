Social Democrat August Bebel: biography, features of activity and interesting facts

Politician and writer August Bebel was born February 22, 1840 in the German city of Cologne. He was the son of a poor non-commissioned officer. Father died of tuberculosis when the boy was still very young. The widowed mother moved with her child to the Hessian city of Wetzlar. There, August Bebel went to school.

Education

At age 14, the future socialist began to learn turning skills. His working day lasted 14 hours. In short periods of free time, the teenager read a lot, swallowing book after book. His favorite works were the novels Robinson Crusoe and Uncle Tom's Cabin. The last book was devoted to the problem of slavery in America. Therefore, even the literary taste clearly demonstrated the young Bebel's aversion to social injustice.

Having learned, the future writer began to travel a lot. Wanderings threw him to various places, but, in the end, he settled in Leipzig. During his travels, August Bebel gained many impressions that shaped him as a person. He had to work as a traveling apprentice during the long period of reaction of the authorities that came after the 1848 revolution.





The beginning of public activity

Just when August Bebel began to live in Leipzig (1860), a revival of political life began to be traced throughout Germany. The number of demonstrations and strikes by the unemployed increased. The center of discontent was not only Leipzig, but also Berlin, as well as Elberfeld. In these conditions, labor unions began to appear like mushrooms after rain. In 1861, Bebel Augustus joined the Craft Educational Society.

The organization made the turner famous. He not only studied a lot, but also began to speak regularly in public. Bebel was soon included in the leadership of the company. However, ambition did not allow him to focus on educational activities. In 1866, Bebel, together with Wilhelm Liebknecht, established the Saxon People's Party. Then the politician became chairman of the Council of Workers' Unions.





Principal socialist

In a new position, August Bebel went to rapprochement with the First International. His decision caused heated debate in the party. In the end, it split. In 1869, Bebel stood at the head of the new Social Democratic Labor Party, which became the flagship of leftist ideas in Germany. The activity of the politician was extremely important for all his supporters and like-minded people. This is evidenced even by the fact that Karl Marx considered him the most outstanding leader of German Social Democracy.

As early as 1867, elections were held in the Reichstag of the North German Union, in which August Bebel was elected deputy. The biography of a politician is an example of the life of a person who fought to the end for his views. In the midst of the war against France, Bebel delivered a fiery speech in which he called for peace with the French for the sake of the solidarity of the workers of all countries. For this, the speaker was tried for treason. At the Leipzig trial, Social Democrat August Bebel was sentenced to two years in prison.





Persecution

In prison, the politician was much engaged in self-education, so even he could spend his imprisonment to good use. Bebel was soon released and continued to fight for the rights of workers. In 1878, he was expelled from Leipzig, which had become his family. The reason for the repression of power was the "Exceptional Law against Socialists." This document, signed by Kaiser Wilhelm I, prohibited the party activities of leftists outside parliament.

Bebel began to live in Borsdorf. He continued to travel around the country and carry out semi-legal party work, for which he was sentenced twice more to short prison sentences. Publications of the 70s and 80s showed who August Bebel is from an ideological point of view. He was a staunch supporter of Karl Marx and his ideas set forth in Capital. Bebel opposed revisionism in the then German socialism, which aimed to revise the foundations of the left doctrine.

Anti-militarist

In 1889, the Second International was established. The activities of the last years of Bebel's life were mainly connected with this organization. Companions in the Social Democratic movement from around the world entrusted him with the most important tasks. Bebel, if health allowed, always participated in congresses of the international. His speech in 1904 at the congress in Amsterdam was especially striking.

And in 1907, in Stuttgart, Bebel again, as in his youth, severely criticized the supporters of militarism. The Russian emigrant Vladimir Lenin was also present at that congress. Death and Heritage

Bebel died on August 13, 1913 in the Swiss city of Passugge. According to the will of the politician, he was buried in Zurich. His departure was mourned not only in Germany, but also in many other countries of the world. Rallies in memory of the socialist were held in Russia, America and even Australia. Obituaries to the defender of the proletariat were published in all working newspapers.
