Palazzo Ducale. Doge's Palace in Venice

All over the world there is a small number of historical buildings or structures, which, for all their masterpiece, do not fit into the framework of orthodox stylistic definitions. These buildings are original symbols of cities, countries or religious denominations. Doge's Palace in Venice falls into this category of iconic architectural masterpieces. It is his image that most often arises in the mind when it comes to Venice. In a sense, its creators were counting on such an effect.



Doge's Palace in Venice. His story and style

This palace was obliged to make a strong impression on everyone who sees it. It was intended to live and perform administrative and state functions to all future rulers of Venice. And this unique city-state reached a high degree of power and influence by the beginning of the fourteenth century. They were ruled by elected rulers - Doges. And the Doge's Palace in Venice was obliged to emphasize their status. In addition to the supreme rulers, many other state structures were to be located here: the Senate and the Grand Council, the Supreme Court and the secret police. Material resources made it possible to solve this problem. The city was the richest in the entire eastern Mediterranean. Doge's Palace in Venice was built over a quarter century. The best masters and artists of the era worked on its construction. Professional secrets of craftsmanship in the Middle Ages were carefully guarded from outsiders and transmitted only within their clan or family dynasty, from father to son. That is why some construction and finishing technologies remain unsurpassed to this day.

To make sure of this, just look at what the Doge's Palace in Venice looks like inside. The luxury and expressiveness of its interiors are amazing. It organically corresponds to the general style of the entire architectural concept. And the palace building was included in all world architecture books as the brightest example and the highest rise of Italian Gothic architecture, which immediately preceded the Renaissance.
