Batteries for the camera: overview, description and types, specifications, selection tips

It is no secret that the productivity of the photographer depends not only on the quality of the equipment, but also on its uninterrupted power. Actually, everything is simple - there is no charge, no pictures. Therefore, before leaving for the next photo shoot, it is clearly not out of place to check your professional baggage for the presence of batteries for the camera.

Moreover, modern, as well as advanced, technology is very demanding on batteries. Here, the smooth operation of the flash must be ensured, and the normal brightness of the screen, and the work of the "filling". So the choice of batteries for the camera must be approached very carefully. Moreover, today's market offers a lot of options, as they say, for every taste and color.

So, let's try to figure out which batteries for the camera are best suited to what to pay special attention in the first place and how not to miscalculate with the purchase. Consider the main characteristics of batteries and what they are remarkable for when it comes to the field of photography.

Difficulty choosing

To begin with, we will conduct a short educational program on the types and types of batteries for the camera. On sale you can find two types of such batteries, and they differ from each other very significantly. The first are simple and disposable batteries for the camera. That is, there is no question of any recharging of batteries. They are directly from the store shelf ready to work and at times cheaper than their more productive counterparts.

Batteries

The batteries for the camera are designed for many recharging cycles. Despite its high cost, such batteries are more practical in terms of not only efficiency, but also payback. After purchase, they usually need charging, after which they can be used as ordinary batteries.

Non-standard batteries (branded)

There are also specific and non-standard batteries. They are equipped with proprietary equipment, it is almost impossible to find an alternative to them. For example, a good half of Sony cameras have a built-in battery and only changes due to normal wear and tear or damage. So here you can not even think about replacing the batteries.

Chemical composition

Batteries may also vary in chemical composition. A little earlier varieties of the chemical "filling" were a dime a dozen, but until today only four have survived, which can boast of an acceptable return for modern technology.

Ni-cd

Nickel-cadmium batteries are the most common batteries. They are relatively inexpensive, well resistant to subzero temperatures and have consistently high charging currents. This option also begins to slowly become obsolete, because the cons sometimes outweigh the pros.

As a fly in the ointment, the toxicity of the elements and a decrease in capacity with each recharging are here. In addition, such batteries must be charged only after complete depletion. Otherwise, the operational period of the elements will be significantly reduced.

Ni-MH

Nickel-metal hydride batteries can be called an improved version of nickel-cadmium. They retained all the advantages of the previous generation and practically got rid of its shortcomings. Regular recharges here now have a lesser effect on the operating life, and the toxicity of the batteries has been reduced.

As for the operation, the efficiency of such batteries has doubled, and the voltage is stably maintained and does not change even with a minimum charge. It is also worth noting that nickel-metal hydride batteries can have significantly higher capacities than conventional nickel-cadmium batteries.

Li-ion

Lithium-ion batteries are completely devoid of the effect of a decrease in capacity when recharging, they are light in weight and high in density. The main advantages of batteries of this type are the lowest self-discharge and fast recharge. In addition, the battery offers high discharge voltage.

Lithium-ion batteries are good in many ways, but they have their own, especially for the photographer working in the field, critical shortcomings. Here we are talking about high sensitivity to the environment: low, as well as high, temperatures extremely adversely affect the condition and effectiveness of the battery.

In addition, batteries of this kind do not tolerate full charge and glut. That is, you can‚Äôt leave them for the night. It is also worth noting that the cost of lithium-ion batteries is significantly higher than the same Ni-MH.

Li-Pol

Lithium polymer batteries are a more advanced version of lithium-ion. They have a 20% higher charge density and are noticeably better resistant to ambient temperature.

In fact, the only drawback of lithium-polymer batteries is the high risk of fire during a charge oversaturation or overheating. So on a hot sunny day it is better to leave them discharged at home.

Summarizing

Professional photographers and experts in this field consider nickel-metal hydride (Ni-MH) batteries to be the best batteries for the camera. They are as versatile as possible, are much cheaper than lithium-ion and polymer, and also have sufficient capacity to service almost any serious photographic equipment.

As for manufacturers, here the situation is more or less clear. The same pros and experts recommend paying attention to proven and well-established brands, as well as the series. Beginners can sometimes offer quite worthy and inexpensive options, but you have to sort through them with great care.
