Microfinance organizations were born in Russia in the early 90s. In the last century, they enjoyed relatively little popularity. Microfinance companies were in a free graze until 2010, when a law was finally passed at the federal level, which allows regulating relations between organizations providing microfinance services and the population.

What is an MFI and what is it eaten with?

MFIs are financial organizations that provide microloans to the public. We are talking about small monetary favors granted for a certain period, with the return of pre-determined interest for using them. The return period is from a minimum of 5 days to a year. But the most popular period through which the population repays debt is a month.





Appearance story

The year of birth of microfinance in the world is considered to be 1976, when the Grameen Bank was founded by Professor Mohammed Yunus. He specialized in microloans to poor Bangladeshis.

Much later, services such as micro deposits and microinsurance were added to the existing ones. This financial assistance was designed for people with low incomes. Today, around the world there are about 10,000 microfinance organizations that serve more than 20 million people in third world countries and in developing countries.





The difference between MFIs and banks

The main difference between an MFI and a bank is its high loyalty to its potential borrowers. For microfinance organizations, it is not a problem for a client to have a dysfunctional credit history, while for banks, this item is very important. MFIs are issued to all groups of the population - students, retirees, officially unemployed citizens, and even those who have nothing to confirm their income with.

Banks and MFIs are not competing companies. These are rather complementary structures - the proposals of banks and MFIs vary. Banks offer long-term loans with optimally selected interest, and MFIs - small cash loans for a short time.





Cost of interest for use

Given that when issuing cash loans, any credit institution is at risk, it tries to foresee this. To minimize cash loss, MFIs put their risk in the interest on the loan.

The daily interest rate on a microloan averages 2%, and the annual, respectively, 730%. Citizens who are negatively opposed to MFIs, as a rule, emphasize this in their reasoning. But in the calculations, it should be remembered that the period for which a loan is taken is usually about a month. The main purpose of the loan is to reach the next financial payments, so the interest for use does not seem very high.
