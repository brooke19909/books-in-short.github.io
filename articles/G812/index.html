Shkolnoye Lake in Zelenograd: ode to the Waterman

In 1703, at the behest of Peter the Great, a city called St. Petersburg was laid down at the mouth of the Neva River. After nine years, he became the capital of Russia and carried this proud title for more than two centuries. The construction was difficult, because there were only swamps all around. And no roads. Nothing but a river. But despite the bitter impassability, the city developed and required considerable attention. In 1843, the construction of the Nikolaev railway begins. She was to connect Peter and Moscow with the help of steam locomotives. Outlandish at the time for many citizens of cars. The road was vital and, above all, for the development of industry. And the steam engines, releasing smoke from their pipes, began to ply between the cities, spreading goods, and at the same time passengers.

Steam engine and "Water pump"

But this strange beast is so arranged that it needs water to work. Warming up and turning into steam, she actually was the driving force of a huge roaring machine. And the road is long ... Where can I get this steam engine? Therefore, artificial reservoirs began to appear along the railway. One of them was built in 1861 on the territory of modern Zelenograd.

And the name was given - "Vodokachka pond." Before the advent of electric locomotives, all steam boilers of railway transport were filled with life-giving moisture here. Like every pond, Vodokachka, and later Shkolnoye Lake in Zelenograd, has its own history. There were fables about him. Often with a mystical color, which was reinforced by the grimy and cheerful stokers. That was before the war.

School Lake in Zelenograd, post-war time

Many old-timers remembered the lake as a fabulous place, it was so beautiful there. Around him was a forest of centuries-old pines and young firs. The children bathed in the summer in spite of all parental prohibitions, as if this water attracted and attracted. Time passed, the war passed. The most terrible and bloody for this land. In the postwar period, the lake has changed little.

Here are just the tops of fir cut by shells, and the ditches reminded of past battles. All the same frolic boys and girls, birds sang the same way. And silence ... And also boats that were easily tied to a small pier with oars at the bottom. Everyone could ride on the lake. And as a rule, returning, they left the boats in the same place, on the pier.
